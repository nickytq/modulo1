Braille Alphabet

How does it work?

The Braille system works through a cell made up of six raised dots organized into two columns and three rows. Each dot has a specific position numbered from 1 to 6, which allows different combinations to be created. Depending on which dots are raised, letters, numbers, punctuation marks, and even musical symbols can be represented. This structure makes the system very efficient because only six dots can create dozens of different characters. In addition, Braille is read using the fingertips, allowing people with visual disabilities to quickly recognize each symbol through touch. The simplicity of its design makes learning and writing easier, either manually or through specialized devices such as Perkins machines or electronic Braille displays. Thanks to this organization, Braille adapts to different languages and needs, becoming a universal tool for communication and access to information.

The position of the dots is numbered as follows:

  • 1
  • 2
  • 3
  • 4
  • 5
  • 6
